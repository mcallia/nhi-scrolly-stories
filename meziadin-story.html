1 · Where you are

Start with the whole map

This is the country around Meziadin Lake, in Gitanyow territory at the headwaters of the Nass — about 220 km north of Terrace, where Highway 37A splits off toward Stewart. The small map in the corner shows where you are in BC.

The orange dashed line is the proposal on the table right now. Keep scrolling and we'll take it a piece at a time.

2 · The lake

Deep, cold, and full of fish

Meziadin Lake is one of the most productive sockeye nurseries in the province. Cold, clear creeks come off the glaciers and empty into it, and the sockeye that hatch here feed communities and businesses all the way down the Nass.

75% of Nass sockeye start in this watershed
3% — its share of the Nass watershed by area

If you've driven Highway 37 to Stewart, you've passed right through this map.

3 · Already protected

The green part has a head start

The Hanna–Tintina Conservancy — the green area — has protected Hanna and Tintina creeks since 2013. BC and the Gitanyow set it up because those two creeks held the biggest sockeye spawning beds in the watershed.

It was the right line for where the fish were spawning in 2013. The fish, it turns out, had other plans.

4 · The gap

The fish are moving west

As Bear Glacier pulls back, Strohn Creek is running clearer and slightly warmer — and the sockeye have noticed. A radio-tagging study found 41% of returning Nass sockeye heading for Strohn Creek, a creek with no protection of any kind.

The orange area is the fix: a 47,000-hectare westward expansion taking in Strohn Creek, Surprise Creek, and the other cold tributaries the run is shifting toward.

5 · Watch the glacier

The line is drawn for where the fish are going

This corner of the map explains the whole proposal. Bear Glacier sits just off Highway 37A; its meltwater built Strohn Creek. The 2013 boundary was drawn for where the fish were. This one is drawn for where they're going.

That's the part worth remembering when someone asks why the existing conservancy isn't enough.

6 · The whole picture

One boundary around the nursery

Put together, the existing conservancy and the expansion make one continuous protected area wrapped around the lake and every major creek that feeds it.

71,000hectares protected in total (existing + expansion)
60.8km of glacier-fed streams inside the line

Proposed jointly by the BC government, Gitanyow First Nation, and Nisga'a Nation.

What "protected" actually means here

This isn't a park with a gate. It's a line on the map that decides what kind of work happens inside it — and the uses that define this country stay in.

Conservancy, n.

A protected area under BC's Park Act, set up jointly with First Nations. It protects habitat and cultural use while keeping room for recreation and low-impact business. It is the same designation Hanna–Tintina has had since 2013 — this proposal extends it.

Stays: huntingfishingsnowmobilingheli-skiingguiding & tourismHighway 37 & 37Acommunity access
Goes: mining & mineral explorationcommercial logginglarge-scale hydrooil & gas

No operating mine or mill is displaced — there are none in the plan area. Existing mineral tenures would be retired with compensation as required by law, and 89% of the surrounding Nass South planning area stays open to mineral exploration.
